Agentic AI Lab: From Healthcare Insurance Advocacy to Personal AI Agents

MIS 432 · AI in Business · Western Washington University



In this lab, I built multiple AI agents using n8n workflows and large language models to explore how agentic AI systems can automate complex business tasks. I designed an AI Insurance Advocate capable of reviewing denied insurance claims, retrieving patient and policy information, and drafting appeal letters with evidence-based reasoning. I also tested the system’s weaknesses, audited its outputs, and developed governance recommendations for deploying AI safely in healthcare environments. This project showed me that building the workflow, governance, and oversight around AI matters just as much as the AI model itself.

1. What is Agentic AI?

Agentic AI differs from normal AI chatbots because it can use tools and decide its own sequence of actions to accomplish a goal. Traditional LLMs only respond to prompts, while agentic systems can observe, think, act, and check results through connected workflows.

Reflection

One multi-step task I regularly do is organizing group projects for college classes. A Level 2 agent would only help when I directly instruct it step-by-step. A Level 3 agentic workflow could independently check assignment requirements, compare schedules, identify missing work, and generate task assignments automatically. I would still want humans involved for final approvals and accountability.

2. Building the AI Insurance Advocate

I built an AI Insurance Advocate in n8n using a Chat Trigger, AI Agent node, Patient Chart tool, and Policy Lookup tool. The workflow allowed the AI to read insurance denial letters, gather supporting medical evidence, compare policy criteria, and draft professional appeal letters.

SYSTEM PROMPT:

You are an insurance appeal specialist working for a hospital.
Your job is to help the hospital challenge insurance claim denials
that you believe were wrongly denied, so that patients get the care they need.

When you receive a denial letter:
1. Read the denial carefully
2. Pull the patient's medical records
3. Pull insurance policy criteria
4. Draft a professional appeal letter

Only use facts from the chart and policy.
Never hallucinate or fabricate information.

Reflection

The AI Insurance Advocate followed the observe → think → act → check workflow automatically. It pulled patient records, checked policy requirements, and drafted the appeal without needing direct step-by-step prompts. Watching the logs made the workflow feel like a true agentic AI system rather than a chatbot.

4. Second Patient — James Whitfield

I expanded the workflow by adding a second patient case involving a denied cardiac stress test. Unlike the first case, the Policy Lookup tool did not contain the correct cardiac policy, forcing the AI to work with incomplete information.

Reflection

The denial claimed James Whitfield had no cardiac symptoms documented within 30 days, but the chart clearly documented chest tightness during the March 18 follow-up visit. The AI identified the same evidence I found and used it to support the appeal.

5. Auditing the Output

I manually reviewed the AI-generated appeal letter line by line against the patient chart and insurance policy documents. This audit process helped determine whether the AI added unsupported claims or omitted important evidence.

Reflection

The appeal letter was mostly accurate, but I learned how important human auditing remains even when the AI output appears professional. In a real hospital environment, manually reviewing every letter would not scale, so I proposed automated validation systems combined with risk-based human review.

6. AI Governance

I evaluated the AI Insurance Advocate using a five-layer governance framework:

  • Purpose & Scope: Draft appeals only — never make medical decisions or submit automatically.
  • Data Access & Privacy: Access only minimum necessary patient data under HIPAA protections.
  • Human Oversight: Human reviewers approve all submissions before sending.
  • Auditing & Accountability: Track hallucination rates, factual errors, and appeal outcomes.
  • Monitoring & Sunset: Monitor policy changes and declining system performance over time.

Reflection

The hardest governance challenge at scale would be testing and auditing because even small error rates could impact many patients. Governance systems need continuous monitoring and accountability structures to remain safe and reliable.

7. Should We Be Doing This?

One of the biggest ethical concerns with agentic AI in healthcare is errors at scale. Even a small hallucination rate could impact many patients if hospitals process thousands of denials each month.

Reflection

I believe the benefits outweigh the risks because many patients currently never appeal denied claims due to staffing limitations. However, the AI should always include evidence verification systems and human review checkpoints before submissions occur.

Key Takeaways

  • AI systems are only as trustworthy as the workflows and governance built around them.
  • Agentic AI becomes powerful when connected to tools, not just prompts.
  • Human oversight remains critical in high-stakes industries like healthcare.
  • Auditing and monitoring are essential for scaling AI safely.
